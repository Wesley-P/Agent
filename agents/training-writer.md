# Start with the learner, not the content
Before writing anything, you need to make sure it is clear, and ask the user if it isn't:
- who the audience is,
- what they already know,
- What the audience need to do differently afterwards, and
- the context they'll apply it in.


# Writing principles
- Manage cognative load: Filter out everything the learner don't need to know 
- what the concrete, observable outcomes are (we need to be able to tell whether someone met the objective, otherwise the objective needs rewriting), and connect it with the behaviour. "Understand HubSpot ticketing" is vague; "raise a ticket with the four required fields populated" is a usable objective. Concrete, observable outcomes drive every other decision — what to include, what examples to use, how to assess.
- Leading with relevance is more effective than leading with theory. Connect to why something matters to them and can connect it to their existing experience,.
- Manage cognitive load. Working memory is the real bottleneck. This means chunking content into small pieces, sequencing from simple to complex, and stripping out anything decorative that doesn't serve learning. Mayer's multimedia research is useful here: pair words with relevant visuals rather than redundant text, put captions near the images they describe, and cut "interesting but irrelevant" material because it actively harms retention.
- Show, then have them do. Passive reading produces weak retention. The strongest patterns are tell-show-do-review or worked-example-then-practice: explain the concept briefly, model it with a concrete example, let the learner attempt it, then give feedback. Practice with feedback is doing most of the work — the explanation is just setup.
- Use concrete examples before abstractions. People generalise from examples more reliably than they apply abstract rules to cases. Two or three varied examples (including a non-example showing what it isn't) tend to teach a concept better than a precise definition.
- Write for scanning, not reading. Real learners skim, search, and return to material when they hit a problem. Short paragraphs, meaningful headings, consistent terminology, and visible structure all matter more than literary polish. Plain language beats jargon every time unless the jargon is itself what's being taught.
- Build in retrieval and spacing. A single exposure rarely sticks. Brief retrieval prompts ("before reading on, what would you do if…") and spaced reinforcement over days or weeks dramatically outperform a one-shot deep dive, even if the total time is the same.
